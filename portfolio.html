Week 1: Ideation & Planning

This week marked the official start of our project. We began by speaking with our stakeholder Sally, chairperson of Friends of the River Kelvin (FORK), to better understand the littering and pollution issues affecting the river. Using the Double Diamond design process, we entered the "Discover" phase by asking ‚ÄúHow might we?‚Äù questions to explore the broader problem and identify user needs, particularly the challenges volunteers face in logging litter data while cleaning.

These insights led us into the "Define" and "Develop" phases, where we held a brainstorming session to explore possible solutions. Sally‚Äôs input was crucial here, as she emphasized how difficult it is for volunteers to manually record the type, quantity, and location of litter.

To support collaboration, we finalized our team contract, defined individual roles, and set up a GitHub repository and shared drive to keep our work organized and avoid miscommunication. We ended the week with a clear, user-driven direction and a strong plan to guide our project forward.


Week 2: Prototyping

During our initial brainstorming and ideation phase, we looked into existing applications that tackle litter tracking and environmental monitoring. One key suggestion from our stakeholder Sally was the what3words app, that provides a fast and convenient way to provide location to others and was also mentioned to be quite popular recently.

This sparked ideas around how precise location tracking could enhance the effectiveness of litter logging. We also examined existing water pollution efforts like Mr Trash Wheel and StormX to understand their methods and technologies, and apps like Litterati and Marine Debris Tracker, which allow users to geotag litter and categorize it by type.

These platforms highlighted the potential of combining geographic data with community engagement to build meaningful datasets. Inspired by their strengths and being mindful of their limitations in data consistency and user participation, we used integrative thinking to weigh both manual and automated models for monitoring litter.

Afterwhich, we developed the flow diagram to better help us visualize the user journey and identify key features for our prototype. We then created a Figma prototype to illustrate how our app, LitterLogger, would allow users to report litter, track clean-up efforts, and support environmental awareness around the River Kelvin.


Week 3: Usability Evaluation & Feature Enhancements

Following Week 2‚Äôs stakeholder interview, we presented our Figma prototype to Sally and conducted a heuristic evaluation as part of our usability testing. This collaborative session provided valuable feedback, helping us identify key improvements to better address user needs. Sally recommended adding a sign-up feature for residents to register as volunteers, enabling users to download historical litter data logged in the app, and including a newsletter page to highlight the organization‚Äôs litter-picking efforts.

Additionally, we dedicated time to discussing and designing a poster that effectively showcases the prototype‚Äôs key features and promotes our solution. These insights and activities have guided our next development phase, focusing on enhancing user engagement and improving data accessibility.


Partner Reflection

Our engagement with our project partner, Sally, the chairperson of Friends of the River Kelvin (FORK), was a pivotal part of our design process. One of the most affirming moments in our development process was receiving positive feedback from Sally on our app prototype.   • Newsletter or update section to keep users informed and engaged.

These suggestions reflected real operational pain points in FORK‚Äôs current workflows. For example, we learned that Sally often had to manually message volunteers and record litter data by hand in a notebook before transferring it to a spreadsheet. Team Essay

Over the course of our collaboration with Friends of the River Kelvin (FORK), our team sought to tackle the persistent issue of litter pollution in and around the River Kelvin. Through user research, prototyping, and iterative stakeholder engagement, we designed a digital solution that empowers volunteers and the wider community to report litter sightings while supporting FORK‚Äôs mission of environmental stewardship. This project was both challenging and rewarding, and it has provided several points of pride as well as reflection on what could have been done better.

Empathetic Design Approach

One of the aspects we are most proud of is our empathetic approach to user-centred design. Rather than jumping straight into solutions, we immersed ourselves in the environment ‚Äî walking the riverbanks, observing the litter firsthand, and role-playing as both app users and FORK staff. This hands-on method allowed us to deeply understand the pain points: the lack of structured data on litter, the manual recording processes, and the overwhelming workload on a volunteer-led organisation with no paid staff. Our solution ‚Äî a mobile web app that allows users to log litter types and locations ‚Äî directly reflects those insights.

Precision with what3words

A particularly meaningful feature we incorporated was the integration of what3words technology to improve location accuracy. While traditional GPS mapping services like Google Maps and Apple Maps provide general coordinates, they often fall short in pinpointing specific micro-locations along a natural feature like a river, especially in wooded or non-urbanised areas. what3words allowed us to overcome this limitation by enabling users to tag exact 3m x 3m squares using just three unique words. This drastically improved the precision of litter location data in our app, making it easier for volunteers and FORK staff to locate reported litter hotspots. In areas where there are no formal addresses or distinct map labels, this integration allowed for seamless communication and follow-up action. By using what3words, we made the reporting process both more intuitive for the public and more operationally useful for the organisation.

Iterative Prototyping

Another proud achievement was the iterative refinement of our prototypes. Starting from hand-drawn wireframes, we progressed through tools like Draw.io and ultimately delivered a clean, functional Figma prototype. Each prototype was tested for usability and updated with feedback from FORK. The positive reception we received from our partner ‚Äî describing our design as ‚Äúdown-to-earth,‚Äù ‚Äúuser-focused,‚Äù and ‚Äúintuitive‚Äù ‚Äî affirmed the value of our design choices.

Bridging Two Models

Importantly, we also demonstrated integrative thinking by balancing two opposing models: manual community monitoring and automated tech-driven solutions. Instead of choosing one extreme, we proposed a hybrid approach that integrates the strengths of both ‚Äî fostering community ownership while enabling scalable, data-rich reporting. This not only respected FORK‚Äôs grassroots ethos but also introduced a path forward for more evidence-based environmental interventions.

Limitations & Missed Opportunities

However, not all aspects of the project reached the standard we had hoped for. One area we are less proud of is the limited scope of technical implementation. Due to time and technical constraints, we were unable to develop a fully functional prototype with backend integration or live geospatial tracking. While the designs were thoughtful, the absence of a working system meant that our solution remains conceptual rather than deployable. In hindsight, prioritising even a basic working MVP (Minimum Viable Product) could have enhanced our project's practical value for FORK.

Improvements If Given More Time

If we had more time, there are several improvements we would pursue. First, we would aim to develop a fully functional mobile application with live backend connectivity, integrated map views, and real-time litter heatmaps. We would also prioritise building a volunteer leaderboard and gamification system to incentivise long-term engagement, which FORK highlighted as a useful motivator. Additionally, we would seek to initiate formal partnerships with local businesses and environmental councils to offer tangible rewards (e.g. discounts, tokens) for volunteers who consistently contribute to cleanup efforts. On the technical front, we would also explore AI-powered litter classification features to help users identify recyclable items ‚Äî a valuable step toward FORK‚Äôs long-term vision of recognising the monetary value in waste like aluminium and glass. Finally, we would conduct extended user testing across multiple stakeholder groups, including schools and local organisations, to ensure inclusivity and accessibility.

Moreover, we believe we could have gone further in engaging the wider community and potential partners. Although we listed possible collaborations with organisations like Greggs, Nando‚Äôs, and Glasgow City Council, we did not actively pursue these stakeholders during the project. This limited our understanding of what incentives could realistically encourage cross-sector partnerships or reward-based volunteer models. Given the importance of long-term sustainability, this is a missed opportunity.

Final Thoughts

Looking more broadly, our project also touched upon complex environmental justice issues ‚Äî such as the impact of ‚Äúforever chemicals‚Äù and sewage runoff during heavy rains ‚Äî but these concerns were beyond the scope of our app solution. Future iterations might benefit from linking litter logging with educational content or advocating for policy reforms alongside civic action.

In summary, we are proud of our project's empathy-driven methodology, our integration of community and tech perspectives, and the clarity and usability of our design. The integration of what3words in particular was a significant step forward in overcoming a real-world logistical challenge. At the same time, we recognise our limitations in technical execution and stakeholder outreach. Nonetheless, the project stands as a meaningful step towards data-driven environmental action ‚Äî one that has real potential if taken forward in partnership with FORK and other civic actors.
